Game Project

A Journey to Babylon

Role Gameplay Designer & Artist
Engine Unity
Duration 20 Weeks
Date Oct 2024 – Mar 2025
Summary

A Journey to Babylon is a 2D narrative RPG built in Unity over a 20-week capstone, combining exploration with turn-based combat. The work spanned art and enemy design, gameplay, and a structured QA pass of 20 dedicated test sessions targeting visual clarity, input latency, and the accuracy of in-game text across UI and narrative.

Overview

A Journey to Babylon follows Rosalie Nehrai, a young traveler crossing the ancient forest realm of Silva Aetherea toward the distant Kingdom of Babylon to fulfill a promise made to her late mother: to "find something more." Chapter 1, Autumn's Rest, opens as a mysterious purple rift tears across the night sky, and what begins as a quiet stop in a sleepy village turns into a fight for its survival when monsters of unknown origin lay siege after dark.

The world is grounded in original lore: Silva Aetherea, the "Ethereal Forest" once revered as sacred; the prosperous Kingdom of Babylon, built on the discovery of Magic Stones that channel the elements at a cost to the wielder's mind; and Autumn's Rest, a waypoint village along the Silk Road. Built by a multi-disciplinary capstone team across writing, art, design, UI/UX, and programming, the Chapter 1 vertical slice brought together exploration, dialogue with NPCs, and a turn-based combat scene.

Role & Scope

The work on this project sat across three areas:

  • Art & enemy design. Produced enemy sprites and combat-scene assets, contributing to the visual language of the encounters players face in Autumn's Rest.
  • Gameplay. Helped shape the turn-based combat scene, including the encounter layout (player on the right, enemies on the left) and the flow between exploration and combat states.
  • Text & QA. Reviewed and revised in-game text for clarity, tone consistency, and readability across both UI and narrative, and ran the QA pass on the build.

QA Approach

With a narrative game, two kinds of quality matter at once: the build has to function, and the writing has to read cleanly in-context. The QA pass targeted both:

  • Ran 20 dedicated QA sessions across the Chapter 1 build, focused on visual clarity, input latency, and text accuracy.
  • Reviewed narrative and UI text in-engine, catching tone inconsistencies and readability issues that only surface once dialogue is actually on screen, not in the script.
  • Logged issues with steps to reproduce, severity, and expected-vs-actual outcomes, then re-tested after each patch to catch regressions.
  • Flagged build-flow issues for the team, such as scene-transition triggers and player spawn placement in the combat handoff.

The QA pass kept the Chapter 1 slice stable and readable through to its build deadline, surfacing and resolving issues with visual clarity, input response, and on-screen text before they reached the demo. The text review tightened tone and readability across the UI and the game's branching dialogue, so the writing landed as intended in-engine.

Lessons Learned

Narrative QA is its own discipline. Bugs in a turn-based system are relatively easy to reproduce and file, but text issues — a line that reads fine in a script but feels off in a dialogue box, or UI copy that's unclear under pressure — only show up when you test the writing in-context, on the actual screen. Treating the text as something to QA rather than just proofread caught problems that a code-only pass would have missed.
